Street Food History – How it All Started

by Dominique Doughty | Nov 28, 2016 | News

For many people, understanding the various forms of street food history is likely to be fairly confusing. Despite being something that many of us are so utterly used to seeing, the history of street food is actually a little more diverse than you may have expected. Street food history is embedded into our culture. We often eat street food but never seem to question its origins.

Typically, street food is a form of meal made on the street and in clear public places – it’s cheaper than restaurant and fast food, and is usually sold on the go. It could be from a food truck or cart; it could be from a kiosk – it could be from a table!

Street food is some of the most common eating in the world today, with over 2.5bn people taking part in enjoying a variety of treats as they make their way through the bustling cities of the world. From hot dogs and pretzels to hamburgers and falafel, there’s an opportunity to get a little bit of something on any street corner!

What is Street Food?

Ready to eat food, or street food, has a long and winding history that stems back many civilizations – indeed, likely back to the time of the Ancient Greeks! Street vendors then sold small portions of fried fish, whilst the Ancient Romans ate street food for nutrition due to a lack of home ovens back in the era.

The most common form of street food is a local meat in some form of bread, usually seen as the ideal way to eat for most people on the go. From hot dogs in a bun in the United States to cheese buns in Brazil, there are many ways to get access to fantastic street foods.

Taco is probably the most popular street food, and is the most commonly searched for of all the street food going on internet search histories.

Street Food History

Street food history takes us centuries back. China has been using street food for many years, conceptualized as a way to feed the poor but even those who were well-off would enjoy a little delicacy from time to time. Of course, they would send a servant to get it for them!

Egyptian street vendors go back as far as the 14th Century, when you could easily pick up lamb kebab, rice and even fritters with relative ease. Going back even further, the ancient Aztec civilizations made use of Atole, a gruel made from maize, as well as tamales with various forms of meat prepared for the people in question.

Further West, European nations have been using this kind of food for some time. 19th Century Parisians were able to enjoy French Fries from street vendors, whilst those in Romania were enjoying cookies, corned cream and even bacon. In London, you could get anything from tripe to pea soup and jellied eels!

Today, street food is far more globalized. Go to London and you can find foods from every continent – the days of nationalized street foods is beginning to end, replaced by a street food industry that everyone can make the most of.
